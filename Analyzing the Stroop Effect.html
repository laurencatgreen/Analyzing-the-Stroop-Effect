Introduction¶

In this experiment, the Stroop effect was investigated for a sample group of people who took part in an experiment. In a Stroop experiment, participants are given a list of words in an ink colour and the participant's task is to say the colour of the ink the word is printed in out loud, regardless of what the printed word says.

There are two conditions: a congruent words condition and an incongruent words condition. For the congruent case, the ink colour of the words displayed match the meaning of the word printed, for example RED, BLUE. For the incongruent words condition, the ink colour of the words do not match the word meaning, for example PURPLE, ORANGE. In each case, the time taken to name the ink colours using equally sized lists is recorded.

What is the independent variable? What is the dependent variable?

An independent variable is the variable that is changed or controlled in an experiment. In this case, the independent variable is the test condition, whether the words condition are congruent or incongruent.

The dependent variable is the variable that is tested in an experiment; a change in the independent variable causes a change in the dependent variable. The dependent variable for this experiment is the time taken to complete the experiment, in other words the time taken to complete naming the ink colours of the words.

Hypotheses¶

Our null hypothesis will be that there is no difference in the recorded reading times for the congruent and incongruent word conditions. In other words, the population mean reading time for the incongruent condition is equal to the population mean reading time for the congruent condition.

The alternative hypothesis is that there is a difference in the time taken to read text that is displayed as incongruent than the reading time for text that is displayed as congruent. The population mean reading time for the incongruent and congruent conditions are different.

The null and alternative hypotheses are as follows, in notation:

$$\mu_{diff} = \mu_{I} - \mu_{C}$$

$$H_0: \mu_{diff} = 0$$

$$H_1: \mu_{diff} \neq 0$$

where,

$\mu_{I}$ and $\mu_{C}$ are the population mean reading times for the incongruent and congruent word conditions, respectively. $\mu_{diff}$ is the population mean difference between the two word conditions.

If $\mu_{diff}$ is much greater than zero (a large positive number), then we can say that the reading time is longer for the incongruent word condition than the reading time for the congruent word condition.

If $\mu_{diff}$ is significantly less than zero, we can conclude that the reading times for the incongruent word condition is shorter than the reading time for the congruent word condition.

Statistical t-test¶

We are given a sample size of n = 24. Below is a figure that depicts when to use a t-test versus a z-score.

We therefor use a t-test to analyze the two sample means (congruent and incongruent) as we have a small sample size that is less than 30 and the population standard deviation is unkown for our sample.

A t-test is used to tell if there is a significant difference before and after results or if the difference could have been by chance. Usually t-tests are used to test if means are different. A larger t-value suggests a larger difference in the two samples.

A two-tailed paired/ dependent t-test for means will be conducted. This type of t-test is usually used when your data has two observations from the same group. Here we have the same group of people recording the mean time to say the words out loud for two test conditions (congruent and incongruent word cases) for a sample size of n = 24. Thus we assume that our sample is dependent and therefor it is possible to pair them. We would like to test if there is any difference in the mean reading/ reaction times for the congruent and incongruent cases.

Dependent T-Test¶

Now, perform the statistical test and report the results. What is the confidence level and your critical statistic value? Do you reject the null hypothesis or fail to reject it? Come to a conclusion in terms of the experiment task. Did the results match up with your expectations?

We use a dependent or paired t-test as our sample is the same group of people conducting the experiment with two differenct test conditions (congruent and incongruent). A 95% confidence interval was assumed, $\alpha = 0.05$ (a 5% statistical significance level).

# We need a list/array instead of a column in a dataframe for a t-test
congruent_list = df['Congruent'].tolist()
incongruent_list = df['Incongruent'].tolist()

# T-test for two dependent samples
t_test = stats.ttest_rel(incongruent_list, congruent_list)

print('t-statistic = {}'.format(t_test[0]))
print('p-value = {}'.format(t_test[1]))

t-statistic = 8.020706944109957
p-value = 4.103000585711178e-08

From the dependent t-test, we found that the t-statistic was 8.02 and the p-value was 0.00000004.

We reject the null hypothesis (that there is no difference in the recorded reading times for the congruent and incongruent word conditions) since the p-value is much less than 0.05 (using a 95% confidence interval). Thus, we accpect the alternative hypothesis that that there is a difference in the time taken to read text that is displayed as incongruent than the reading time for text that is displayed as congruent. This result matches what was expected before the statistical test was conducted that the incogruent case would have longer reading times than the congruent case.

We could also use the t-statistic to get the same result as using the p-value. We find the t-critical value using t-tables or Graphpad, using 23 degrees of freedom and $\alpha = 0.05$ (95% confidence interval). The t-critical value was found to be greater than 2.069 or less than -2.0687. Since our t-statistic of 8.02 is past this critical value, again, we reject the null.

Conclusion¶

In conclusion, using a two-tailed dependent samples t-test, it was found that there is that there is a statistical difference in the time taken to read text that is displayed as incongruent than the reading time for text that is displayed as congruent.

Furthermore, it was found that on average, the reading times for the incongruent word conditions had longer reading times than that of the congruent word conditions. A potential explanation for this that when we have to recognise the colour of the word but the word description is different to the colour, there is an interference in the reaction time, causing a longer reading time. This interferance can be explained by the following theories:

  • Speed of Processing Theory: interference can be explained because words are read faster than colors can be named,
  • Selective Attention Theory: interference occurs as naming colors requires more attention than reading words.
